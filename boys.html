The biggest trend that jumped out from these graphs
was the difference in longevity between girls' and boys' names.
Male names like James and William ranked in the top 10 in 1936
and in 2016. Other names like Michael spent decades on top of the
rankings. The girls' names didn't see this consistency. The top 10
lists in 1936 and 2016 were totally different, and the downward and
upward trajectory of the names were very stark.

When I delved deeper into the
data, I found that the overall
number of female names given each
year was consistantly higher than
the number of male names. This
shows that there is a relative lack
of variety in boys' baby names.

Names like William, a perennial
favorite for new parents, are
both classic and clearly not seen
as outdated or old-fashioned over
the years. This is how I think names
like William consistently out-rank
names like Nicholas (my name).

Visualizing this baby name data revealed a lot of surprising
trends in world of child-naming. While it's still unclear what
goes into making the perfect popular name, we are now free to
take this data and draw our own conclusions from it.
