It’s hard to say, but thanks to the U.S Social Security
Administration (SSA), we can trace the popularity of baby
names over the years and decades. A pertinent question
to ask of this data is: Do popular names even stay popular?
Which names have been hot for years and which ones
were just a passing fad?

The U.S government began
recording baby names at the
turn of the 20th century but
the process was loosely ran
and decentralized. Very few
babies’ birth data and names
ended up in the hands of the
federal government until
the year of 1936.

It was in this year that the
SSA began recording the
names assigned at birth as
part of the larger goal of
establishing the system of
Social Security. Without this
data the mission of tracking
popular baby names would
be very difficult.

Between 1936 and 2016,
millions of baby names were
recorded, giving us a daunt-
ing amount of data to sift
through, however with the
help of the programming
language R, the process was
actually fairly smooth.

The results of my experi-
mentation were really quite
surprising. Some names
rose and fell in groups, oth-
ers bucked the trend. There
were different behaviors of
boys names versus girls.
Take a look for yourself!

Once I had the data wrangled, it was time to choose
how to organize the names. I decided to take the top
10 baby names in 1936 and track their progression in the
name popularity rankings all the way up to 2016. Then,
I took the top 10 names in 2016 and tracked their popul-
arity back in time to 1936. this was done for both boys and
girls in separate visuals.
